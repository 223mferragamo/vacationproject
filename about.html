HISTORY

of Saint Martin

The Beginnings of Saint Martin's History

It is believed that the first groups of Meso-Indians who came from Venezuela and the Yucatan Peninsula. Soon after, many other trips arrived searching for more Caribbean islands. However, during the first hundred years, these indigenous populations were killed during the Spanish conquest of the Americas.

Through history, this helped speed up their history and make Saint Martin a land with constantly changing influences and lots of arrivals of different populations.



Discovery of the Island

On November 11, 1493, during his second voyage, Christopher Columbus sailed past the island he would then name Saint Martin, after the patron saint.

About 200 years later, the island was divided into two when the land was shared between the French and the Dutch. Splitting the land between the two nations was done with no conflict and benefited both of them.



The Land of Salt

During the era of pirates and privateers, many different nations wanted Saint Martin's salt reserves, giving it the nickname Soualiga, or the land of salt.

The Dutch were the first to find the salt pond of Great Bay, leading to Dutch families coming to Saint Martin and marking the beginning of its story.



The Treaty of Concordia

On March 23, 1648, the French and the Dutch both signed the Treaty of Concordia, also known as the Partition Treaty. This treaty officially split the island into two parts, one owned by the French, and the other by the Dutch. The French own the Northern part of the island with about 20 mi² of land, while the Dutch own the southern part with about 13 mi² of land.

It is believed that the dividing line was determined by two men on a walk, one Frenchman, and one Dutchman. They stood back-to-back and began walking opposite directions along the coast. This mutually created the border, although there is no physical border between the two nations.
